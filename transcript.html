Interview Transcript with Dr. Logan Smilges

Logan Smilges: My name is Logan Smilges, and when I publish, I usually tack on the 1st initial of my 1st name J, and my research interests hover around queer and trans disability studies, rhetorical studies, and the history of medicine.

Logan Smilges: My first book, “Queer Silence,” was based on my dissertation. I spent a good deal of my adolescence in conversion therapy, which offered a harmful but comprehensive lens for thinking about sexuality and disability. Grad school helped me make sense of that experience. The book evolved into an exploration of queerness, disability, and how language shapes embodiment.

Logan Smilges: I discuss apps like Grindr as technologies through which bodies are negotiated. Technology can mediate, extend, or even become part of the body—like with hearing aids or communication tools. For disabled people, tech is often essential to embodiment and community building.

Logan Smilges: Expectations vary widely on queer apps. While some seek hookups, others seek connection, safety, or identity affirmation. The same platform may be experienced as liberating or oppressive depending on these expectations—and people often list preferences that reflect deeper systems of exclusion.

Logan Smilges: Yes, when relevant. I emphasize to students the importance of articulating their position in relation to the topic. Sharing my lived experiences—rather than just identities—helps foster deeper engagement and critical reflection.

Logan Smilges: I center marginalized voices in my syllabi and design assignments that encourage students to see their own relationships to power. I don’t treat “diversity” as a checklist—I aim to reframe it as a lens for intellectual rigor and structural analysis.

Logan Smilges: Silence is not absence—it’s a multimodal, gestural presence. I use silence as a teaching tool, allowing space for reflection, and I write about citation as a rhetorical silence—deferring to others who speak with greater authority.

Logan Smilges: Silence online can be strategic or fearful. Students may feel disempowered or disengaged. We need to read silence in context, rather than make blanket assumptions about its meaning or value.

Logan Smilges: Grindr makes some silences possible (like visual anonymity), but punishes others. People without visible bodies or profiles may be ignored. Silence becomes both tool and barrier depending on social expectations baked into the platform.

Logan Smilges: I’m interested in how people stay connected in digital space amid rising fascism and social media collapse. I don’t know exactly how my work fits in the digital humanities, but I’m drawn to questions of survival, collectivity, and future building through tech.
